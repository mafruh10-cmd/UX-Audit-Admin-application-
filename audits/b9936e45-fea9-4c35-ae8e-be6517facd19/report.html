Detailed Findings
Issue 01WCAG 1.4.1 · A
Failed payment status communicated by color alone
📍 Failed payments panel — red 'Failed' badge on each row
High
The Problem
All three failed payment entries use only a red-colored badge with the word 'Failed' to communicate status. Users with color vision deficiency (protanopia/deuteranopia) cannot distinguish this badge from neutral states if the color is their primary differentiator, and screen readers receive no additional context about the severity or required action beyond the word itself. There is no icon, pattern, or supplementary affordance to reinforce the status independently of color.
Why critical: WCAG 1.4.1 explicitly prohibits using color as the only visual means of conveying information. Psychologically, loss aversion (Kahneman) means failed payment states are high-stakes; failing to communicate them clearly causes disproportionate user anxiety and missed recovery actions.
Recommendation
Add a warning icon (e.g. ⚠) inside or adjacent to each 'Failed' badge so status is conveyed through both color and shape. Ensure the badge also has an accessible name via aria-label that includes context such as 'Payment failed — action required'. Consider adding a short inline CTA like 'Retry' to reduce the gulf of execution.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.1 Use of Color (A): color must not be the only visual means of conveying information
Issue 02WCAG 4.1.2 · A
Icon-only toolbar buttons lack accessible text labels
📍 Top-right navigation — grid, help, bell, settings, and plus icon buttons
High
The Problem
The five icon-only buttons in the top-right corner (apps grid, help circle, bell, settings gear, plus) have no visible text labels, meaning their purpose is entirely opaque to new users and completely inaccessible to screen reader users if alt text or aria-labels are absent. Without a visible or programmatic label, keyboard and AT users cannot determine where each button leads before activating it.
Why critical: WCAG 2.4.6 requires headings and labels to be descriptive, and 4.1.2 requires name, role, and value for all UI components. The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) identifies this as a Signifier failure — visible cues must indicate where and how to act.
Recommendation
Add visible text labels below each icon, or at minimum provide persistent tooltips that appear on hover and focus. Ensure every icon button has a descriptive aria-label (e.g. aria-label='Open settings') so screen reader users receive equivalent information. Group related icons with a nav landmark and a visually hidden heading.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (A): all UI components must have an accessible name; The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Signifier: visible cues must indicate where to act
Issue 03WCAG 1.4.3 · AA
Secondary metadata text likely fails minimum contrast ratio
📍 Failed payments panel — date and email text (e.g. '6 Dec, 00:41 • iamleonardkim@gmail.com')
High
The Problem
The timestamp and email metadata beneath each payment amount appears in a light gray color against a white background. This combination visually estimates below the WCAG AA threshold of 4.5:1 for normal-sized text, making it difficult to read for users with low vision or in bright-light environments. This data is functionally important — it identifies which customer and when each failure occurred.
Why critical: WCAG 1.4.3 requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text at AA level. Designing User Interfaces (Malewicz) highlights that reduced contrast on data-dense dashboards disproportionately affects legibility for aging users and those with visual impairments.
Recommendation
Darken the secondary metadata text to at minimum #767676 on white (which achieves 4.54:1), or use #595959 for comfortable margin. Validate with a contrast checker tool such as WebAIM's contrast checker before deployment. Avoid using opacity-based color reduction as a substitute for a properly specified color token.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.3 Contrast Minimum (AA): normal text requires 4.5:1 contrast ratio
Issue 04
Failed payments panel offers no recovery action — dead end UX
📍 Failed payments widget — three MYR 2.00 failed rows
High
The Problem
All three displayed failed payments show identical amounts (MYR 2.00), timestamps, and customer email with no inline action to retry, investigate, or dismiss. The only escape path is 'View more' which leads to a list — not a resolution flow. Users experiencing payment failures are in a high-anxiety, time-sensitive state and need immediate actionable affordances.
Why critical: Loss aversion (Kahneman / Psych 101 Kleinman) means negative financial events receive disproportionate attention. The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) identifies a Gulf of Execution here — users cannot determine how to resolve the problem from the current view, increasing frustration and error recovery time.
Recommendation
Add a 'Retry' or 'Investigate' quick-action button or link on each failed payment row, or surface a summary CTA such as 'Retry all 3 failed payments' at the bottom of the widget. Consider a distinct visual treatment for the widget header (e.g. an orange or red accent border) to signal urgency while conforming to accessibility requirements by pairing with an icon.
Reference: The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Gulf of Execution: user cannot figure out how to do what they want; Psychology of Design — Loss Aversion: negative states demand clear recovery paths
Issue 05WCAG 1.1.1 · A
New customers graph lacks axis labels and data point annotations
📍 New customers chart — line graph spanning Jan 2024 to Dec 2024
Medium
The Problem
The line graph shows only two Y-axis labels (0 and 3) and two X-axis labels (Jan 2024 and Dec 2024), with no intermediate gridlines, data point markers, or hover tooltips visible. The steep spike at December has no annotation explaining the cause or the exact value, making it impossible for users to interpret the data distribution across the year.
Why critical: Universal Principles of Design — Signal-to-Noise Ratio: a chart that hides its own data points maximises noise and minimises signal. Working memory research (Miller's Law) indicates users cannot retain or compare unlabelled data points, forcing unnecessary re-examination.
Recommendation
Add interactive tooltips on data points showing the exact customer count and date on hover/focus. Include intermediate Y-axis gridlines and at minimum quarterly X-axis markers. Ensure the chart is keyboard navigable and that data is also available in a tabular format as an accessible alternative.
Reference: Universal Principles of Design — Signal-to-Noise Ratio: maximise relevant information, minimise clutter; WCAG 2.2 — 1.1.1 Non-text Content (A): charts must have a text alternative
Issue 06
Invoice widget shows mostly MYR 0.00 values without contextual explanation
📍 Invoices panel — Draft, Paid, Uncollectible, Void rows all showing MYR 0.00
Medium
The Problem
Four of the five invoice categories display MYR 0.00 while only 'Open' shows a non-zero value. New users cannot determine whether these zeros represent an empty account state, a filtered view, or a data loading issue. No empty state message, onboarding hint, or tooltip clarifies the situation.
Why critical: Don't Make Me Think (Krug) — users should never have to wonder whether something is a result or an error state. Zero-value displays without context create ambiguity that increases cognitive load and erodes trust in the data's accuracy.
Recommendation
Add a contextual empty-state message beneath zero-value rows when appropriate (e.g. 'No paid invoices yet'). Alternatively, collapse zero-value rows by default and show them only on expansion, reducing visual noise. Include a last-updated timestamp per category or a global data-freshness indicator beyond the single 'Updated yesterday' label.
Reference: Don't Make Me Think (Krug) — Billboard Design 101: users scan, not read; Universal Principles of Design — Progressive Disclosure: show only what is needed at each step
Issue 07
'Test mode' toggle lacks clear consequence labelling
📍 Top navigation bar — 'Test mode' toggle switch (top right)
Medium
The Problem
The 'Test mode' toggle is displayed without any indication of what activating or deactivating it does to the data shown in the dashboard, or whether it is currently on or off (the visual state of the toggle is ambiguous at this resolution). Users could mistakenly believe live data is being shown when test data is active, or vice versa, leading to incorrect business decisions.
Why critical: The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Gulf of Evaluation: users cannot tell what state the system is in or the consequence of their action. Nielsen's Heuristic #1 (Visibility of System Status) requires that users always be informed about what is going on.
Recommendation
Add a persistent banner or badge across the dashboard when test mode is active (e.g. a yellow top bar reading 'Viewing test data — not your live account'). Ensure the toggle has a visible on/off state with both a color change and a text label update (e.g. 'Test mode: ON'). Include an aria-checked attribute for screen reader state communication.
Reference: The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Gulf of Evaluation: user cannot tell if their action worked; Nielsen's Heuristic #1: Visibility of System Status
Issue 08
Top customers widget shows only one entry with no comparison context
📍 Top customers by spend panel — single entry 'Leonard Kim, MYR 2.00'
Medium
The Problem
The 'Top customers by spend' widget displays only one customer (Leonard Kim at MYR 2.00) with no rank, percentage of total revenue, or comparison to other customers. A widget titled 'Top customers' implies a ranked list, but showing one entry provides no competitive or analytical value and wastes the panel's real estate.
Why critical: Psychology of Design — Hick's Law and expectations set by labelling: when a label promises a ranked list, showing one item violates the user's mental model and forces unnecessary cognitive re-evaluation of what the widget is for. Don't Make Me Think (Krug) warns that mismatched labels and content erode user trust.
Recommendation
Show at least 3–5 ranked customer entries within the widget to fulfil the 'Top customers' promise, or rename the widget to 'Highest spending customer' if only one entry is intended. Add a percentage-of-total-revenue column or sparkline to give the figure meaning beyond an isolated monetary value.
Reference: Don't Make Me Think (Krug) — Billboard Design 101: labels must match content; Psychology of Design — Hick's Law: user expectations shape decision speed
Issue 09WCAG 1.4.1 · A
Outstanding invoices use color-only category differentiation
📍 Outstanding invoices panel — colored dot indicators for overdue categories
Low
The Problem
The outstanding invoices breakdown uses small colored dots (purple, blue, teal, orange) as the sole differentiator between overdue categories. Users with color vision deficiency cannot reliably distinguish between teal and blue dots, or purple and blue, when all amounts are MYR 0.00 and the only visual cue is the dot color.
Why critical: WCAG 1.4.1 prohibits using color as the only visual means of conveying information. Gestalt Law of Similarity (Psych 101, Kleinman) means users will group identically-shaped dots together, relying entirely on color to parse categories — which fails for approximately 8% of male users with color blindness.
Recommendation
Replace or supplement the colored dots with distinct icons or patterns (e.g. a clock icon for overdue, a warning triangle for >60 days). Alternatively, add a short textual abbreviation inside or beside the indicator (e.g. '>60d', '30–60d'). Ensure dot size meets a minimum of 24×24px for touch accessibility.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.1 Use of Color (A): color must not be the only visual means of conveying information; Psych 101 (Kleinman) — Gestalt: Law of Similarity
Issue 10
Sidebar navigation lacks visible active-state differentiation for sub-items
📍 Left sidebar — Products section (Payments, Billing, Reporting, More)
Low
The Problem
The sidebar Products items (Payments, Billing, Reporting, More) are rendered without a visible active/selected state indicator at a glance, and collapsed items with chevrons offer no preview of their contents. Users navigating the dashboard cannot immediately orient themselves within the product hierarchy without trial-and-error expansion.
Why critical: Nielsen's Heuristic #6 (Recognition Over Recall) states that users should not have to remember which section they are in — the UI must show it. Psych 101 (Kleinman) Gestalt figure-ground principle requires that the selected item stands out clearly from its background.
Recommendation
Apply a persistent left-border accent or background highlight to the currently active sidebar item and its parent group. Add abbreviated content previews or tooltips to collapsed chevron items so users can anticipate what is inside before expanding. Ensure focus indicators meet WCAG 2.4.11 minimum focus appearance requirements.
Reference: Nielsen's Heuristic #6: Recognition Rather Than Recall; Psych 101 (Kleinman) — Gestalt: figure-ground differentiation; WCAG 2.2 — 2.4.11 Focus Appearance (AA)
