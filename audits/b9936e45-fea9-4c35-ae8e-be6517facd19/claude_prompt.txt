Overall Score:        6.4/10  —  Good

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
The dashboard provides a reasonable overview of key metrics but suffers from several critical accessibility failures including reliance on color-only status indicators, insufficient contrast in secondary text, and icon-only action buttons lacking text labels. UX issues compound this with a repeated failed-payments pattern that lacks actionable recovery guidance, and an invoice section that shows MYR 0.00 across most categories without contextual explanation, increasing cognitive load for users trying to interpret their financial state.

ISSUES TO FIX  (all issues must be addressed — ordered by severity)
====================================================================

[01] HIGH  —  Failed payment status communicated by color alone
     Location:       Failed payments panel — red 'Failed' badge on each row
     Problem:        All three failed payment entries use only a red-colored badge with the word 'Failed' to communicate status. Users with color vision deficiency (protanopia/deuteranopia) cannot distinguish this badge from neutral states if the color is their primary differentiator, and screen readers receive no additional context about the severity or required action beyond the word itself. There is no icon, pattern, or supplementary affordance to reinforce the status independently of color.
     Fix:            Add a warning icon (e.g. ⚠) inside or adjacent to each 'Failed' badge so status is conveyed through both color and shape. Ensure the badge also has an accessible name via aria-label that includes context such as 'Payment failed — action required'. Consider adding a short inline CTA like 'Retry' to reduce the gulf of execution.
     Reference:      WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.1 Use of Color (A): color must not be the only visual means of conveying information
     WCAG:           1.4.1  —  Level A

[02] HIGH  —  Icon-only toolbar buttons lack accessible text labels
     Location:       Top-right navigation — grid, help, bell, settings, and plus icon buttons
     Problem:        The five icon-only buttons in the top-right corner (apps grid, help circle, bell, settings gear, plus) have no visible text labels, meaning their purpose is entirely opaque to new users and completely inaccessible to screen reader users if alt text or aria-labels are absent. Without a visible or programmatic label, keyboard and AT users cannot determine where each button leads before activating it.
     Fix:            Add visible text labels below each icon, or at minimum provide persistent tooltips that appear on hover and focus. Ensure every icon button has a descriptive aria-label (e.g. aria-label='Open settings') so screen reader users receive equivalent information. Group related icons with a nav landmark and a visually hidden heading.
     Reference:      WCAG 2.2 — 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (A): all UI components must have an accessible name; The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Signifier: visible cues must indicate where to act
     WCAG:           4.1.2  —  Level A

[03] HIGH  —  Secondary metadata text likely fails minimum contrast ratio
     Location:       Failed payments panel — date and email text (e.g. '6 Dec, 00:41 • iamleonardkim@gmail.com')
     Problem:        The timestamp and email metadata beneath each payment amount appears in a light gray color against a white background. This combination visually estimates below the WCAG AA threshold of 4.5:1 for normal-sized text, making it difficult to read for users with low vision or in bright-light environments. This data is functionally important — it identifies which customer and when each failure occurred.
     Fix:            Darken the secondary metadata text to at minimum #767676 on white (which achieves 4.54:1), or use #595959 for comfortable margin. Validate with a contrast checker tool such as WebAIM's contrast checker before deployment. Avoid using opacity-based color reduction as a substitute for a properly specified color token.
     Reference:      WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.3 Contrast Minimum (AA): normal text requires 4.5:1 contrast ratio
     WCAG:           1.4.3  —  Level AA

[04] HIGH  —  Failed payments panel offers no recovery action — dead end UX
     Location:       Failed payments widget — three MYR 2.00 failed rows
     Problem:        All three displayed failed payments show identical amounts (MYR 2.00), timestamps, and customer email with no inline action to retry, investigate, or dismiss. The only escape path is 'View more' which leads to a list — not a resolution flow. Users experiencing payment failures are in a high-anxiety, time-sensitive state and need immediate actionable affordances.
     Fix:            Add a 'Retry' or 'Investigate' quick-action button or link on each failed payment row, or surface a summary CTA such as 'Retry all 3 failed payments' at the bottom of the widget. Consider a distinct visual treatment for the widget header (e.g. an orange or red accent border) to signal urgency while conforming to accessibility requirements by pairing with an icon.
     Reference:      The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Gulf of Execution: user cannot figure out how to do what they want; Psychology of Design — Loss Aversion: negative states demand clear recovery paths

[05] MEDIUM  —  New customers graph lacks axis labels and data point annotations
     Location:       New customers chart — line graph spanning Jan 2024 to Dec 2024
     Problem:        The line graph shows only two Y-axis labels (0 and 3) and two X-axis labels (Jan 2024 and Dec 2024), with no intermediate gridlines, data point markers, or hover tooltips visible. The steep spike at December has no annotation explaining the cause or the exact value, making it impossible for users to interpret the data distribution across the year.
     Fix:            Add interactive tooltips on data points showing the exact customer count and date on hover/focus. Include intermediate Y-axis gridlines and at minimum quarterly X-axis markers. Ensure the chart is keyboard navigable and that data is also available in a tabular format as an accessible alternative.
     Reference:      Universal Principles of Design — Signal-to-Noise Ratio: maximise relevant information, minimise clutter; WCAG 2.2 — 1.1.1 Non-text Content (A): charts must have a text alternative
     WCAG:           1.1.1  —  Level A

[06] MEDIUM  —  Invoice widget shows mostly MYR 0.00 values without contextual explanation
     Location:       Invoices panel — Draft, Paid, Uncollectible, Void rows all showing MYR 0.00
     Problem:        Four of the five invoice categories display MYR 0.00 while only 'Open' shows a non-zero value. New users cannot determine whether these zeros represent an empty account state, a filtered view, or a data loading issue. No empty state message, onboarding hint, or tooltip clarifies the situation.
     Fix:            Add a contextual empty-state message beneath zero-value rows when appropriate (e.g. 'No paid invoices yet'). Alternatively, collapse zero-value rows by default and show them only on expansion, reducing visual noise. Include a last-updated timestamp per category or a global data-freshness indicator beyond the single 'Updated yesterday' label.
     Reference:      Don't Make Me Think (Krug) — Billboard Design 101: users scan, not read; Universal Principles of Design — Progressive Disclosure: show only what is needed at each step

[07] MEDIUM  —  'Test mode' toggle lacks clear consequence labelling
     Location:       Top navigation bar — 'Test mode' toggle switch (top right)
     Problem:        The 'Test mode' toggle is displayed without any indication of what activating or deactivating it does to the data shown in the dashboard, or whether it is currently on or off (the visual state of the toggle is ambiguous at this resolution). Users could mistakenly believe live data is being shown when test data is active, or vice versa, leading to incorrect business decisions.
     Fix:            Add a persistent banner or badge across the dashboard when test mode is active (e.g. a yellow top bar reading 'Viewing test data — not your live account'). Ensure the toggle has a visible on/off state with both a color change and a text label update (e.g. 'Test mode: ON'). Include an aria-checked attribute for screen reader state communication.
     Reference:      The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Gulf of Evaluation: user cannot tell if their action worked; Nielsen's Heuristic #1: Visibility of System Status

[08] MEDIUM  —  Top customers widget shows only one entry with no comparison context
     Location:       Top customers by spend panel — single entry 'Leonard Kim, MYR 2.00'
     Problem:        The 'Top customers by spend' widget displays only one customer (Leonard Kim at MYR 2.00) with no rank, percentage of total revenue, or comparison to other customers. A widget titled 'Top customers' implies a ranked list, but showing one entry provides no competitive or analytical value and wastes the panel's real estate.
     Fix:            Show at least 3–5 ranked customer entries within the widget to fulfil the 'Top customers' promise, or rename the widget to 'Highest spending customer' if only one entry is intended. Add a percentage-of-total-revenue column or sparkline to give the figure meaning beyond an isolated monetary value.
     Reference:      Don't Make Me Think (Krug) — Billboard Design 101: labels must match content; Psychology of Design — Hick's Law: user expectations shape decision speed

[09] LOW  —  Outstanding invoices use color-only category differentiation
     Location:       Outstanding invoices panel — colored dot indicators for overdue categories
     Problem:        The outstanding invoices breakdown uses small colored dots (purple, blue, teal, orange) as the sole differentiator between overdue categories. Users with color vision deficiency cannot reliably distinguish between teal and blue dots, or purple and blue, when all amounts are MYR 0.00 and the only visual cue is the dot color.
     Fix:            Replace or supplement the colored dots with distinct icons or patterns (e.g. a clock icon for overdue, a warning triangle for >60 days). Alternatively, add a short textual abbreviation inside or beside the indicator (e.g. '>60d', '30–60d'). Ensure dot size meets a minimum of 24×24px for touch accessibility.
     Reference:      WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.1 Use of Color (A): color must not be the only visual means of conveying information; Psych 101 (Kleinman) — Gestalt: Law of Similarity
     WCAG:           1.4.1  —  Level A

[10] LOW  —  Sidebar navigation lacks visible active-state differentiation for sub-items
     Location:       Left sidebar — Products section (Payments, Billing, Reporting, More)
     Problem:        The sidebar Products items (Payments, Billing, Reporting, More) are rendered without a visible active/selected state indicator at a glance, and collapsed items with chevrons offer no preview of their contents. Users navigating the dashboard cannot immediately orient themselves within the product hierarchy without trial-and-error expansion.
     Fix:            Apply a persistent left-border accent or background highlight to the currently active sidebar item and its parent group. Add abbreviated content previews or tooltips to collapsed chevron items so users can anticipate what is inside before expanding. Ensure focus indicators meet WCAG 2.4.11 minimum focus appearance requirements.
     Reference:      Nielsen's Heuristic #6: Recognition Rather Than Recall; Psych 101 (Kleinman) — Gestalt: figure-ground differentiation; WCAG 2.2 — 2.4.11 Focus Appearance (AA)
