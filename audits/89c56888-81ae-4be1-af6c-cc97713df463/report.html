Detailed Findings
Issue 01WCAG 1.4.1 · A
Star ratings convey information through color alone
📍 Summary table — Current and Potential columns with star ratings
High
The Problem
The rating system uses filled vs. unfilled yellow stars to communicate scores, relying entirely on color and subtle visual fill differences. Users with color vision deficiencies or low vision cannot distinguish between filled and unfilled stars. No text alternative or numeric value is provided for the star ratings in the Current/Potential columns (only Overall row has numbers).
Why critical: WCAG 1.1.1 requires non-text content to have text alternatives, and WCAG 1.4.1 prohibits using color as the sole means of conveying information. Users who cannot perceive the yellow fill are excluded from understanding the ratings.
Recommendation
Add numeric equivalents next to each star rating (e.g., '4/5'). Alternatively, provide aria-labels like 'Design: 4 out of 5 stars' for screen readers and display the numeric value visually alongside the stars.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.1 Use of Color (A): color must not be the only visual means of conveying information
Issue 02WCAG 1.4.3 · AA
Insufficient contrast on chart axis labels and secondary text
📍 Line chart Y-axis labels (0, 0.35, 0.7, 1.05, 1.4) and X-axis date labels
High
The Problem
The axis labels on the Views Per Visit chart appear in a light gray color against the white/light background, likely falling below the 4.5:1 contrast ratio required for normal-sized text. This makes the chart data difficult to interpret for users with low vision.
Why critical: WCAG 1.4.3 mandates a minimum 4.5:1 contrast ratio for normal text. Insufficient contrast in data visualization labels undermines the entire purpose of the analytics display, as users cannot accurately read the scale.
Recommendation
Increase the axis label color to at least #767676 on white backgrounds to meet the 4.5:1 ratio. Use a consistent dark gray (#595959 or darker) for all chart labels to ensure readability.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 1.4.3 Contrast Minimum (AA): normal text requires 4.5:1 contrast ratio
Issue 03
Bounce Rate metric shown without context or benchmarking
📍 Bounce Rate card showing 86%
High
The Problem
An 86% bounce rate is critically high, yet it is displayed with the same neutral visual treatment as other metrics. There is no color-coding, trend indicator, or contextual benchmark to signal this is a concerning value. Users unfamiliar with analytics may not recognize this requires urgent attention.
Why critical: Per the Signal-to-Noise Ratio principle, critical signals must be visually differentiated from normal information. Without visual salience on alarming metrics, users experience the Gulf of Evaluation — they cannot tell if their site performance is good or bad.
Recommendation
Add conditional formatting — display metrics in red/amber/green based on performance thresholds. Include a small trend arrow or comparison to industry benchmarks (e.g., 'Average: 45%'). Add a tooltip explaining what the metric means and what's considered healthy.
Reference: The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Gulf of Evaluation: user cannot tell if their action worked
Issue 04WCAG 1.1.1 · A
Icon-only action buttons lack accessible labels
📍 Bottom of chat panel — row of small icons (undo, thumbs up, thumbs down, copy, more)
High
The Problem
The action icons below the chat response have no visible text labels and likely lack aria-labels. Screen reader users cannot determine the function of these buttons, and sighted users may also struggle to identify their purpose without tooltips.
Why critical: WCAG 1.1.1 requires all non-text content that is functional to have a text alternative. Icon-only buttons without labels create a Gulf of Execution where users cannot determine the available actions.
Recommendation
Add aria-label attributes to each icon button (e.g., 'Copy response', 'Thumbs up', 'Undo'). Add visible tooltips on hover/focus. Consider adding text labels for the most important actions.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 1.1.1 Non-text Content (A): all functional non-text content must have text alternatives
Issue 05
Split-panel layout creates competing focal points
📍 Left chat panel alongside right analytics panel
Medium
The Problem
The screen simultaneously displays a chat interface with AI recommendations on the left and analytics data on the right, creating two competing contexts. Users must context-switch between the conversational AI suggestions and the analytical data, increasing cognitive load.
Why critical: Per Hick's Law and cognitive load theory, presenting two distinct task contexts simultaneously forces users to split attention and increases decision time. The chat panel's improvement suggestions compete with the analytics data for user focus.
Recommendation
Consider collapsing the chat panel when the Analytics tab is active, or provide a toggle to show/hide it. Alternatively, integrate the AI insights directly into the analytics view as contextual annotations near relevant metrics.
Reference: Psychology of Design — Hick's Law: too many choices slows decisions
Issue 06
Views Per Visit chart lacks data point labels and interactivity cues
📍 Line chart in the center of the analytics panel
Medium
The Problem
The line chart shows a spike around Dec 29 but provides no hover tooltips, data point markers, or exact values. Users cannot determine precise values without interacting, and there's no visual affordance suggesting the chart is interactive.
Why critical: Per Norman's Signifier principle, visual cues must indicate where and how to interact. Without data labels or hover states, users face the Gulf of Evaluation — they can see a trend but cannot extract precise information.
Recommendation
Add visible data point markers at each date interval. Implement hover/focus tooltips showing exact values and dates. Add a subtle 'hover for details' hint or make the chart's interactivity discoverable through cursor changes.
Reference: The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Signifier: visible cues must indicate where to act
Issue 07WCAG 2.4.7 · AA
No focus visible indicator apparent on selected metric card
📍 Views Per Visit metric card (highlighted with orange border)
Medium
The Problem
The Views Per Visit card appears selected with an orange border, but it's unclear if this styling would persist as a focus indicator for keyboard users. No other cards show focus styling, suggesting keyboard navigation may not provide visible focus indicators across all interactive elements.
Why critical: WCAG 2.4.7 requires that keyboard focus indicators are visible. If users navigate metric cards via keyboard, they must see which card is currently focused to maintain orientation and control.
Recommendation
Ensure all interactive metric cards display a visible 2px+ focus ring when navigated via keyboard. Use CSS :focus-visible to apply a high-contrast outline that is distinguishable from the selected state.
Reference: WCAG 2.2 — 2.4.7 Focus Visible (AA): keyboard focus indicator must be visible
Issue 08
Data tables lack sorting affordances and row hover states
📍 Source/Visitors, Page/Visitors, Country/Visitors, Device/Visitors tables
Medium
The Problem
The four data tables at the bottom display static data without any sorting controls, hover highlights, or clickable row affordances. Users cannot reorder data or drill down into specific sources, pages, or countries to get deeper insights.
Why critical: Per Norman's Affordance principle, interactive elements must communicate how they can be used. Static-looking tables in an analytics context violate user expectations — analytics tools universally allow sorting and drill-down on tabular data.
Recommendation
Add sortable column headers with arrow indicators. Include hover states on rows to signal clickability. Enable drill-down functionality so users can click a source or page to see detailed breakdowns.
Reference: The Design of Everyday Things (Norman) — Affordance: element must communicate how it is used
Issue 09
Inconsistent visual hierarchy between Overall score and category rows
📍 Summary table — Overall row vs. category rows
Low
The Problem
The Overall row uses bold text and numeric values while category rows use star ratings. This inconsistency forces users to mentally translate between two different rating systems within the same table, creating unnecessary cognitive friction.
Why critical: Per Gestalt's Law of Similarity, items that share visual characteristics are perceived as related. Using two different rating formats (stars vs. numbers) in the same table breaks consistency and increases processing effort.
Recommendation
Show numeric scores alongside star ratings for all rows, or convert the Overall row to also use star ratings. Maintain a single, consistent rating visualization throughout the table.
Reference: Psych 101 (Kleinman) — Gestalt: Law of Similarity
Issue 10
Action buttons at bottom of chat lack visual prominence
📍 'Add contact form' and 'Add mobile menu' pill buttons
Low
The Problem
The suggested action buttons appear as low-contrast outline pills that blend with the background. These are the primary CTAs derived from the AI analysis but lack the visual weight needed to drive user action.
Why critical: Per Refactoring UI's hierarchy principles, primary actions should be visually dominant. The current ghost-button styling makes these actionable suggestions appear as secondary or optional, reducing conversion to improvement actions.
Recommendation
Style at least one primary action as a filled button with the brand accent color. Use a stronger border or background fill to create visual hierarchy between the most impactful action and secondary suggestions.
Reference: Refactoring UI — Use color to support hierarchy, not communicate it alone
